Scientists and Their Practices: Real-World Examples

Before we explore the Science and Engineering Practices, let's see how real scientists have used these practices throughout history to make groundbreaking discoveries. These examples show that science isn't just about knowing facts—it's about actively doing science!

Jane Goodall - Primatologist

Famous for: Revolutionizing our understanding of chimpanzees

SEPs in Action:

  • Asking Questions: "Do chimpanzees have personalities? How do they communicate?"
  • Planning Investigations: Lived in the forest for months, developing new observation methods
  • Analyzing Data: Kept detailed field notes, identified patterns in behavior
  • Communicating Information: Shared findings through books, documentaries, and lectures

George Washington Carver - Agricultural Scientist

Famous for: Developing hundreds of products from peanuts and improving soil health

SEPs in Action:

  • Defining Problems: "How can we help farmers whose soil is depleted from growing only cotton?"
  • Developing Models: Created crop rotation systems
  • Designing Solutions: Invented new uses for peanuts, sweet potatoes, and soybeans
  • Using Mathematics: Calculated crop yields and soil nutrient levels

Marie Curie - Physicist and Chemist

Famous for: Discovering radioactivity and two new elements (polonium and radium)

SEPs in Action:

  • Asking Questions: "Why do some materials give off mysterious rays?"
  • Planning Investigations: Tested thousands of mineral samples systematically
  • Analyzing Data: Measured radiation levels, identified patterns
  • Constructing Explanations: Developed the theory of radioactivity
  • Arguing from Evidence: Defended her findings to skeptical scientists

Katherine Johnson - Mathematician

Famous for: Calculating trajectories for NASA space missions

SEPs in Action:

  • Using Mathematics: Performed complex calculations for spacecraft orbits
  • Developing Models: Created mathematical models of Earth's orbit
  • Analyzing Data: Verified computer calculations by hand
  • Designing Solutions: Solved problems for safe astronaut return

Charles Darwin - Naturalist

Famous for: Theory of evolution by natural selection

SEPs in Action:

  • Asking Questions: "Why do the finches on different islands have different beaks?"
  • Collecting Data: Gathered specimens and made detailed observations
  • Developing Models: Created the "tree of life" diagram
  • Constructing Explanations: Developed theory based on evidence
  • Communicating Information: Published "On the Origin of Species"

Try This: Scientist Match-Up

Create cards with scientists' names, their discoveries, and the practices they used. Have students match them up!

Extension for older students: Research a scientist of their choice and identify which SEPs they used

Discussion prompts:

  • "Which practice do you think was most important for each scientist?"
  • "Do you notice any practices that all scientists used?"
  • "How might their discoveries have been different without these practices?"

Reflection: Science is a Human Endeavor

Consider these questions:

  • What surprised you about how these scientists worked?
  • How can sharing these stories help your students see themselves as scientists?
  • Which scientist's approach resonates most with how you like to solve problems?
  • How might you use these examples to introduce SEPs to your students?

Key Takeaways

  • Real scientists use multiple practices together, not in isolation
  • Scientific discoveries take time, patience, and systematic work
  • Scientists come from diverse backgrounds and bring different perspectives
  • The practices we'll explore are the same ones used by professional scientists

Welcome to K-5 Science and Engineering Practices

This self-paced professional development session will guide you through the eight Science and Engineering Practices (SEPs) as they apply to elementary grades K-5. You'll explore how these practices progress across grade bands and discover practical strategies for implementation in your classroom.

Session Objectives

  • Understand the eight Science and Engineering Practices and their importance
  • Recognize the progression of practices from K-2 to 3-5
  • Identify specific student actions for each practice at your grade level
  • Develop strategies for implementing SEPs in your classroom

Why Science and Engineering Practices?

The NRC uses the term "practices" instead of "skills" to emphasize that engaging in scientific investigation requires not only skill but also knowledge specific to each practice. These practices describe behaviors that scientists engage in as they investigate the natural world and that engineers use as they design and build systems.

The Eight Practices

  1. Asking Questions and Defining Problems - The starting point for science and engineering
  2. Developing and Using Models - Creating representations to explain and predict
  3. Planning and Carrying Out Investigations - Systematic exploration and testing
  4. Analyzing and Interpreting Data - Making sense of observations and measurements
  5. Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking - Quantifying and computing relationships
  6. Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions - The products of science and engineering
  7. Engaging in Argument from Evidence - The process of reaching conclusions
  8. Obtaining, Evaluating, and Communicating Information - Sharing and critiquing ideas

Asking Questions and Defining Problems

A practice of science is to ask and refine questions that lead to descriptions and explanations of how the natural and designed world works and which can be empirically tested.

Key Distinction

Science: Formulation of a question that can be answered through investigation

Engineering: Formulation of a problem that can be solved through design

Primary School (K-2)

Asking questions and defining problems in K–2 builds on prior experiences and progresses to simple descriptive questions.

  • Ask questions based on observations to find more information about the natural and/or designed world(s)
  • Ask and/or identify questions that can be answered by an investigation
  • Define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool

Elementary School (3-5)

Asking questions and defining problems in 3–5 builds from K–2 experiences and progresses to specifying qualitative relationships.

  • Ask questions about what would happen if a variable is changed
  • Identify scientific (testable) and non-scientific (non-testable) questions
  • Ask questions that can be investigated and predict reasonable outcomes based on patterns
  • Use prior knowledge to describe problems that can be solved
  • Define a simple design problem with criteria for success and constraints

Try This: Question Sorting Activity

Materials needed: Chart paper, sticky notes, markers

Setup: Create two columns: "Scientific Questions" and "Non-Scientific Questions"

Sample questions to sort:

  • "What happens to a plant if it doesn't get water?"
  • "Why is blue the best color?"
  • "How does temperature affect how fast ice melts?"
  • "Should we have recess every day?"
  • "Which paper airplane design flies the farthest?"
  • "Is chocolate ice cream better than vanilla?"
  • "What materials block sound the best?"
  • "Why do some balls bounce higher than others?"

Extension Activities:

  • K-2: Have students draw pictures of questions they could test
  • 3-5: Students revise non-scientific questions to make them testable
  • Challenge: Students create their own examples of each type

Discussion Points:

  • What makes a question scientific? (Can be tested, has measurable outcomes)
  • How could we change opinion questions into testable questions?
  • Why do scientists need to ask good questions?

Try This: Question Progression Ladder

Create a visual ladder showing question complexity:

  1. Level 1 (K): "What?" questions - What color is the leaf?
  2. Level 2 (1st): "What happens?" questions - What happens when we water the plant?
  3. Level 3 (2nd): "How?" questions - How do plants grow?
  4. Level 4 (3rd): "What if?" questions - What if we change the amount of light?
  5. Level 5 (4-5): "Why?" questions - Why do plants grow toward light?

Implementation: Display this ladder and explicitly teach students to "level up" their questions

  • Teach question stems appropriate for your grade level
  • Model curiosity by sharing your own questions
  • Celebrate when students ask testable questions

Developing and Using Models

A practice of both science and engineering is to use and construct models as helpful tools for representing ideas and explanations. These tools include diagrams, drawings, physical replicas, mathematical representations, analogies, and computer simulations.

Primary School (K-2)

Modeling in K–2 builds on prior experiences and progresses to include using and developing models that represent concrete events or design solutions.

  • Distinguish between a model and the actual object, process, and/or events the model represents
  • Compare models to identify common features and differences
  • Develop and/or use a model to represent amounts, relationships, relative scales, and/or patterns
  • Develop a simple model based on evidence to represent a proposed object or tool

Elementary School (3-5)

Modeling in 3–5 builds on K–2 experiences and progresses to building and revising simple models.

  • Identify limitations of models
  • Collaboratively develop and/or revise a model based on evidence
  • Develop a model using an analogy, example, or abstract representation
  • Develop and/or use models to describe and/or predict phenomena
  • Use a model to test cause and effect relationships

Types of Models in Elementary Science

  • Physical models: 3D representations (e.g., clay models of landforms)
  • Drawings/Diagrams: 2D representations (e.g., life cycle diagrams)
  • Dramatizations: Acting out processes (e.g., modeling Earth's rotation)
  • Mathematical models: Using numbers and symbols (e.g., graphing plant growth)

Try This: Model Progression Activity - Water Cycle

Materials: Paper, crayons, construction paper, cotton balls, plastic bags, water, ice

Grade-Level Progressions:

  • Kindergarten: Draw pictures showing rain, clouds, and sun. Use cotton balls for clouds!
  • 1st Grade: Create a simple diagram with arrows showing water going up and down
  • 2nd Grade: Add labels to diagrams (rain, cloud, sun, ocean)
  • 3rd Grade: Include vocabulary (evaporation, condensation, precipitation)
  • 4th Grade: Show multiple pathways (groundwater, runoff, transpiration)
  • 5th Grade: Create 3D models and explain limitations (e.g., "My model doesn't show temperature changes")

Physical Model Option: Create a water cycle in a bag - put water in a zip-lock bag, tape to window, observe over days

Assessment Questions by Grade:

  • K-2: "Show me in your model where the rain comes from"
  • 3-5: "What parts of the real water cycle are missing from your model?"

Try This: Grade-Specific Model Activities

Kindergarten: Living vs. Non-Living Models

  • Standard: LS.K.1.1 (characteristics of living organisms)
  • Activity: Sort pictures and create a class model showing what living things need
  • Model type: Picture collage with symbols (water drop, sun, food, shelter)

1st Grade: Push and Pull Models

  • Standard: PS.1.1.1 (effect of push/pull on motion)
  • Activity: Use toy cars and ramps to model forces
  • Model type: Physical demonstration with arrows showing force direction

2nd Grade: Sound Vibration Models

  • Standard: PS.2.2.1 (sound produced by vibrating objects)
  • Activity: Create models using rubber bands, boxes, rice on drum
  • Model type: Physical models showing vibrations you can see

3rd Grade: Solar System Scale Model

  • Standard: ESS.3.1.1 (Earth as part of solar system)
  • Activity: Use playground to show relative distances between planets
  • Model type: Physical scale model, discuss what it can/cannot show

4th Grade: Erosion and Weathering Models

  • Standard: ESS.4.2.3 (changes in Earth's surface)
  • Activity: Stream table models, ice wedging demonstrations
  • Model type: Physical models showing processes over time

5th Grade: Human Body Systems Models

  • Standard: LS.5.1.2 (major systems of human body)
  • Activity: Create interactive models showing system connections
  • Model type: 3D models, role-play, diagrams showing interactions

Try This: Model Types Exploration Station

Set up stations where students experience different model types:

Station 1 - Physical Models:

  • Clay volcanoes, solar system mobiles, plant/animal structures
  • Task: Build a model of a familiar object and explain what it shows/doesn't show

Station 2 - Diagram Models:

  • Life cycles, food chains, machine parts
  • Task: Create a diagram of how something works

Station 3 - Acting Out Models:

  • Earth's rotation, particle movement, ecosystem interactions
  • Task: Use your body to show a science concept

Station 4 - Mathematical Models:

  • Graphs of plant growth, weather data, class surveys
  • Task: Turn number data into a visual model

Debrief Questions:

  • "Which type of model was best for showing movement?"
  • "When might a diagram be better than a 3D model?"
  • "What did you learn about your topic by making a model?"

Planning and Carrying Out Investigations

Scientists and engineers plan and carry out investigations in the field or laboratory, working collaboratively as well as individually. Their investigations are systematic and require clarifying what counts as data and identifying variables or parameters.

Primary School (K-2)

Planning and carrying out investigations in K–2 builds on prior experiences and progresses to simple investigations based on fair tests.

  • With guidance, plan and conduct an investigation in collaboration with peers
  • Plan and conduct an investigation collaboratively to produce data
  • Evaluate different ways of observing and/or measuring a phenomenon
  • Make observations and/or measurements to determine if a solution meets a goal
  • Make predictions based on prior experiences

Elementary School (3-5)

Planning and carrying out investigations in 3–5 progresses to include investigations that control variables and provide evidence.

  • Plan and conduct an investigation using fair tests with controlled variables
  • Evaluate appropriate methods and/or tools for collecting data
  • Make observations and/or measurements to produce data as evidence
  • Make predictions about what would happen if a variable changes
  • Test two different models to determine which better meets criteria for success

Key Components of Investigations

Fair Test: Changing only one variable at a time while keeping others constant

Multiple Trials: Repeating investigations to ensure reliable results

Collaboration: Working with peers to plan and conduct investigations

Try This: Investigation Planning Template

Create a classroom poster with these prompts:

  1. Our Question: What do we want to find out?
  2. Our Prediction: What do we think will happen?
  3. What We'll Change: (Independent variable)
  4. What We'll Keep the Same: (Controlled variables)
  5. What We'll Measure/Observe: (Dependent variable)
  6. How Many Times: (Number of trials)
  7. Safety Considerations: How will we be safe?

Grade-Level Scaffolds:

  • K-1: Use picture cards for each step, teacher scribes
  • 2-3: Students draw and label, use sentence starters
  • 4-5: Students write full plans, peer review before starting

Try This: Fair Test Challenge

Materials: Paper airplanes, measuring tape, masking tape

Round 1 - Unfair Test:

  • Let students test paper airplanes however they want
  • Record results on chart
  • Discuss: "Why might our results be different?"

Round 2 - Making it Fair:

  • As a class, identify variables (thrower, throwing force, height, angle)
  • Decide what to keep the same
  • Mark throwing line with tape
  • Demonstrate consistent throwing technique
  • Test again and compare results

Debrief Questions:

  • "How did our results change when we made it a fair test?"
  • "What else could we control to make it even more fair?"
  • "Why do scientists care about fair tests?"

Extensions:

  • Test different airplane designs
  • Graph results
  • Write conclusions based on evidence

Deep Reflection: Building a Culture of Investigation

Current Practice Audit:

  • How often do students plan their own investigations vs. follow prescribed steps?
  • What management concerns hold you back from more open-ended investigations?
  • How comfortable are your students with "failure" or unexpected results?

Student Readiness:

  • Can your students identify variables in everyday situations?
  • Do they understand why we repeat tests?
  • How do they handle disagreements about procedures?

Gradual Release Plan:

  1. Week 1: Teacher demonstrates planning an investigation (think aloud)
  2. Week 2: Class plans together, teacher guides heavily
  3. Week 3: Small groups plan with template, teacher approves
  4. Week 4: Groups plan independently, peer review

Management Strategies:

  • Start with investigations using simple materials (paper, water, everyday items)
  • Establish clear safety protocols and practice them
  • Use "Investigation Licenses" - students earn independence
  • Create material stations to reduce chaos
  • Build in reflection time to reinforce learning over completion

Action Commitment: Choose one simple investigation you'll let students plan next week. What support will they need?

Analyzing and Interpreting Data

Scientific investigations produce data that must be analyzed in order to derive meaning. Scientists use a range of tools to identify significant features and patterns in the data.

Primary School (K-2)

Analyzing data in K–2 builds on prior experiences and progresses to collecting, recording, and sharing observations.

  • Record information (observations, thoughts, and ideas)
  • Use and share pictures, drawings, and/or writings of observations
  • Use observations to describe patterns and/or relationships
  • Compare predictions to what occurred
  • Analyze data from tests to determine if something works as intended

Elementary School (3-5)

Analyzing data in 3–5 progresses to introducing quantitative approaches and multiple trials.

  • Represent data in tables and/or graphical displays
  • Analyze and interpret data using logical reasoning and mathematics
  • Compare and contrast data collected by different groups
  • Analyze data to refine a problem statement or design
  • Use data to evaluate and refine design solutions

Data Representation Progression

K-1: Drawings, tally marks, simple sorting

Grade 2: Picture graphs, simple tables

Grades 3-5: Bar graphs, line plots, more complex tables

Try This: Data Analysis Scaffold

Guide students through data analysis with these questions:

  • "What patterns do you notice?"
  • "What surprised you about the results?"
  • "How do our results compare to our predictions?"
  • "What might explain what we observed?"

Try This: Grade-Specific Data Activities

Kindergarten: Weather Data Collection

  • Standard: ESS.K.1.2 (summarize daily weather conditions)
  • Activity: Use weather symbols to record daily weather for a week
  • Data display: Picture graph with weather symbols
  • Analysis: "Which weather did we have the most?"

1st Grade: Plant Growth Tracking

  • Standard: LS.1.1.1 (needs of different plants)
  • Activity: Measure and record plant growth in different conditions
  • Data display: Simple bar graph using cubes or drawings
  • Analysis: Compare which plants grew tallest

2nd Grade: Matter Investigation Data

  • Standard: PS.2.1.2 (compare amount of water before/after freezing)
  • Activity: Measure water levels, record changes
  • Data display: Before/after comparison charts
  • Analysis: Look for patterns in volume changes

3rd Grade: Force and Motion Data

  • Standard: PS.3.2.2 (compare relative speeds)
  • Activity: Time objects traveling same distance
  • Data display: Data tables and bar graphs
  • Analysis: Calculate differences, rank by speed

4th Grade: Rock Classification Data

  • Standard: ESS.4.2.2 (classify rocks)
  • Activity: Test rock properties, create classification charts
  • Data display: Multi-column data tables with properties
  • Analysis: Sort into categories, find patterns

5th Grade: Ecosystem Population Data

  • Standard: LS.5.2.1 (compare ecosystem characteristics)
  • Activity: Research and graph populations in different ecosystems
  • Data display: Line graphs, complex data tables
  • Analysis: Compare trends, make predictions

Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking

In both science and engineering, mathematics and computation are fundamental tools for representing physical variables and their relationships.

Primary School (K-2)

Mathematical thinking at K–2 progresses to recognizing that mathematics can describe the natural world.

  • Decide when to use qualitative vs. quantitative data
  • Use counting and numbers to identify and describe patterns
  • Describe, measure, and/or compare quantitative attributes
  • Use quantitative data to compare two alternative solutions

Elementary School (3-5)

Mathematical thinking at 3–5 progresses to extending quantitative measurements and using computation.

  • Decide if qualitative or quantitative data are best for determining success
  • Organize simple data sets to reveal patterns
  • Describe, measure, estimate, and/or graph quantities
  • Create and/or use graphs and charts to compare solutions

Mathematics Integration Examples

  • Measurement: Length, mass, volume, temperature, time
  • Data organization: Charts, tables, graphs
  • Pattern recognition: Sequences, cycles, trends
  • Comparison: Greater than/less than, ordering, ranking

Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions

The products of science are explanations and the products of engineering are solutions.

Primary School (K-2)

Constructing explanations in K–2 progresses to using evidence and ideas in constructing evidence-based accounts.

  • Use information from observations to construct evidence-based accounts
  • Use tools and materials to design/build a device that solves a problem
  • Generate and/or compare multiple solutions to a problem

Elementary School (3-5)

Constructing explanations in 3–5 progresses to specifying variables that describe and predict phenomena.

  • Construct an explanation of observed relationships
  • Use evidence to construct or support an explanation
  • Identify the evidence that supports particular points
  • Apply scientific ideas to solve design problems
  • Generate and compare multiple solutions based on criteria

Supporting Student Explanations

Use the CER framework (Claim-Evidence-Reasoning):

  • Claim: A statement that answers the question
  • Evidence: Data that supports the claim
  • Reasoning: Explanation of how evidence supports the claim

Try This: Grade-Specific Explanation Activities

Kindergarten: Sorting Explanations

  • Standard: PS.K.1.1 (classify objects by physical properties)
  • Activity: Sort objects, explain sorting rule using "because"
  • Example: "I put these together because they are all soft"
  • Scaffold: Picture cards showing properties

1st Grade: Movement Explanations

  • Standard: PS.1.1.2 (compare effects of force on motion)
  • Activity: Test different pushes, explain what happened
  • Example: "The big push made it go far because I used more force"
  • Scaffold: Sentence frames with cause/effect

2nd Grade: Life Cycle Explanations

  • Standard: LS.2.1.1 (summarize animal life cycles)
  • Activity: Observe butterflies, explain changes
  • Example: "The caterpillar made a chrysalis because it's time to change into a butterfly"
  • Scaffold: Sequence cards, transition words

3rd Grade: Matter Change Explanations

  • Standard: PS.3.1.3 (explain changes when heated/cooled)
  • Activity: Ice cube races, explain melting differences
  • Full CER: "The ice in the sun melted faster (claim) because it melted in 5 minutes while shade took 15 minutes (evidence). Heat from the sun gave the ice more energy to change from solid to liquid (reasoning)."

4th Grade: Fossil Explanations

  • Standard: LS.4.2.2 (explain how fossils show Earth's past)
  • Activity: Analyze fossil evidence, explain ancient environments
  • Full CER with multiple pieces of evidence and scientific reasoning

5th Grade: Weather Pattern Explanations

  • Standard: ESS.5.1.3 (explain ocean's influence on weather)
  • Activity: Analyze NC weather data, explain patterns
  • Complex explanations with multiple variables and cause-effect chains

Try This: Building Explanation Skills

Progression of Explanation Complexity:

  1. K-1: Simple Because Statements
    • "This happened because..."
    • One cause, one effect
    • Concrete, observable connections
  2. 2-3: Evidence-Based Explanations
    • "I think... because I observed..."
    • Reference specific observations
    • Use measurement data
  3. 4-5: Full CER with Scientific Reasoning
    • Clear claim answering the question
    • Multiple pieces of evidence
    • Scientific principles in reasoning

Sentence Starters by Grade:

  • K-1: "I noticed..." "This happened because..."
  • 2-3: "My evidence shows..." "This proves that..."
  • 4-5: "Based on the data..." "This demonstrates the scientific principle of..."

Engaging in Argument from Evidence

Argumentation is the process by which explanations and solutions are reached.

Primary School (K-2)

Engaging in argument in K–2 progresses to comparing ideas and representations.

  • Identify arguments that are supported by evidence
  • Distinguish between explanations that account for all evidence
  • Analyze why some evidence is relevant and some is not
  • Listen actively to arguments to indicate agreement or disagreement
  • Construct an argument with evidence to support a claim

Elementary School (3-5)

Engaging in argument in 3–5 progresses to critiquing explanations by citing relevant evidence.

  • Compare and refine arguments based on evidence
  • Distinguish among facts, reasoned judgment, and speculation
  • Respectfully provide and receive critiques from peers
  • Construct and/or support an argument with evidence and data
  • Make claims about solutions by citing relevant evidence

Creating a Culture of Scientific Argument

Sentence starters for respectful scientific discourse:

  • "I agree/disagree because..."
  • "The evidence shows..."
  • "Another way to look at this is..."
  • "Can you explain why you think..."

Obtaining, Evaluating, and Communicating Information

Scientists and engineers must be able to communicate clearly and persuasively the ideas and methods they generate.

Primary School (K-2)

Communicating information in K–2 builds on prior experiences and uses observations and texts.

  • Read grade-appropriate texts and/or use media to obtain information
  • Describe how specific images support a scientific idea
  • Obtain information using various texts and text features
  • Communicate information with others using models, drawings, writing, or numbers

Elementary School (3-5)

Communicating information in 3–5 progresses to evaluating the merit and accuracy of ideas.

  • Obtain and combine information from books and reliable media
  • Read and comprehend complex texts to summarize ideas
  • Compare and/or combine across complex texts
  • Combine information in written text with tables, diagrams, charts
  • Communicate scientific information orally and in written formats

Communication Formats

Multiple ways students can communicate their learning:

  • Science notebooks/journals
  • Posters and presentations
  • Digital slideshows
  • Models and demonstrations
  • Videos and podcasts (for older students)

Try This: Grade-Specific Communication Activities

Kindergarten: Show and Tell Science

  • Standard: LS.K.2.1 (compare animal characteristics)

Implementation Strategies

Getting Started with SEPs

  1. Start Small: Choose one or two practices to focus on initially
  2. Be Explicit: Name the practice when students are using it
  3. Model Thinking: Think aloud to demonstrate the practice
  4. Celebrate Growth: Recognize when students improve in using practices

Integration Tips

  • Practices work best when integrated, not isolated
  • Connect practices to real-world scenarios
  • Use familiar contexts before introducing new content
  • Scaffold complexity based on student readiness

Common Challenges and Solutions

Challenge: Students struggle with open-ended questions

Solution: Provide question stems and practice with "what if" scenarios

Challenge: Limited time for investigations

Solution: Use stations, demos, and virtual investigations

Challenge: Managing materials and mess

Solution: Establish clear procedures and assign student roles
